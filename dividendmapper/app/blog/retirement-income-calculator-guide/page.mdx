## What Is a Retirement Income Calculator — and Why Dividend Investors Need One

A retirement income calculator does something most generic pension calculators don't: it models the *income* your portfolio could produce, not just the total value you might accumulate by a target date.

For UK dividend investors, the difference matters. A lump-sum projection tells you how much your portfolio *might be worth*. An income projection tells you how much it could *pay you* — and whether that's enough to live on, supplement a salary, or bridge the gap to a final-salary pension.

The [DividendMapper Retirement Calculator](/tools/retirement-calculator) works this way. You enter your portfolio size, estimate a realistic dividend yield, choose a growth assumption, and select your wrapper types (ISA, SIPP, GIA). It then projects the annual income you could expect under three scenarios, adjusting for the different tax treatment each wrapper receives.

This guide walks through each piece of that output: what the three scenarios mean, how yield and growth interact, how wrapper choice changes the post-tax picture, and — most importantly — how to use the projection in your real-world dividend planning.

## Which UK Accounts Can You Model? (ISA, SIPP, GIA Explained)

The calculator lets you allocate your portfolio across three account types. Getting the split right is important because each wrapper treats dividends differently.

**ISA (Individual Savings Account):** Dividends inside an ISA are completely tax-free. There is no dividend allowance to track, no tax band to worry about, and no reporting on your self-assessment return. For most UK dividend investors, the ISA is the most efficient home for income-producing holdings. The current annual ISA allowance is £20,000 (2026/27 tax year).

**SIPP (Self-Invested Personal Pension):** Dividends inside a SIPP also grow tax-free, but accessing the money follows pension rules. You can typically start drawing from age 57 (rising to 58 from 2028). When you withdraw, 25% is usually tax-free and the rest is taxed as income. The annual allowance is £60,000 (2026/27), though this includes all pension contributions, not just SIPP.

**GIA (General Investment Account):** Dividends in a GIA are taxable. For 2026/27, the dividend allowance is £500 (down from £1,000 in 2023/24 and £2,000 in 2022/23). Above that, basic-rate taxpayers pay 8.75% on dividend income, higher-rate taxpayers pay 33.75%, and additional-rate taxpayers pay 39.35%. A GIA also triggers capital gains tax when you sell holdings above the annual exempt amount (£3,000 for 2026/27).

For a deeper breakdown of how each wrapper affects your tax bill, see the [UK Dividend Tax Guide 2026/27](/blog/uk-dividend-tax-guide). If you're deciding between an ISA and a SIPP for your dividend portfolio, the [ISA vs SIPP for UK Dividend Investors](/blog/isa-vs-sipp-dividend-investors) comparison covers the trade-offs.

## Understanding the Three Scenarios: Bear, Base and Bull

The calculator does not predict the future — it shows a range of plausible outcomes so you can plan for uncertainty rather than assuming a single number.

**Base scenario (3% annual dividend growth):** This assumes your portfolio's dividend income grows roughly in line with long-run UK dividend growth trends. Most investors should treat this as their central planning case.

**Bear scenario (1.5% annual dividend growth):** A slower-growth environment where companies raise dividends more cautiously. This is not a crash scenario — it assumes dividends still grow, just below trend. Use this for conservative planning to check whether your essential expenses would still be covered.

**Bull scenario (5% annual dividend growth):** A stronger-growth environment above historical averages. Use this to understand upside potential, but do not base essential spending decisions on it.

Each scenario starts from the same current portfolio yield and applies the growth rate consistently across the full investment horizon. The key variable is how those growth assumptions compound over time — and how wrapper taxes change the post-income picture differently under each path.

## How Dividend Yield Assumptions Shape Your Retirement Income Projection

The calculator uses two key inputs that work together:

1. **Current portfolio yield** — the weighted average yield of your holdings today (for example, 4.2% on a £200,000 portfolio produces £8,400 in annual dividends at the start).
2. **Assumed annual dividend growth** — how much those dividends increase each year (3% in the Base scenario).

The interaction is straightforward but powerful. A portfolio yielding 4.2% today with 3% annual growth does not simply produce 4.2% of your starting capital forever. After 15 years, the annual dividend payout (before considering wrapper taxes) would be approximately 55% higher than the starting amount — because the *dividends themselves* grow each year, not just the capital.

This is different from focusing on a headline yield number in isolation. A fund that yields 9% today might cut its dividend tomorrow. The calculator's approach assumes sustainable growth from a realistic starting yield, not a speculative chase for the highest headline rate. (For more on why headline yield can mislead, see [Why Headline Yield Can Be Misleading for UK Income Investors](/blog/why-headline-yield-can-be-misleading).)

## Example: Projecting Income for a £200,000 Dividend Portfolio

Let's walk through a concrete hypothetical example.

**Portfolio assumptions:**

| Input | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Current portfolio value | £200,000 |
| Current portfolio yield | 4.2% |
| Investment horizon | 15 years |
| Wrapper split | 50% ISA (£100,000) / 50% GIA (£100,000) |

**Scenarios:**

| Scenario | Growth assumption | Annual income (ISA) | Annual income (GIA, post-tax) |
|----------|-------------------|-------------------|------------------------------|
| Bear | 1.5% | £10,920 | £9,282 |
| Base | 3.0% | £15,408 | £13,097 |
| Bull | 5.0% | £21,168 | £17,993 |

*These are illustrative arithmetic projections for educational purposes. Actual outcomes depend on individual portfolio composition, dividend policies of the companies you hold, and tax changes over the projection period.*

**How to read these numbers:**

In the Base scenario, 15 years of 3% annual dividend growth turns £8,400/year of starting income into approximately £13,088/year in gross dividends before wrapper tax. Inside the ISA, all of that is tax-free — £15,408/year of spendable income. Inside the GIA, after the £500 dividend allowance, the remaining £12,588 is taxed at the basic rate of 8.75%, leaving approximately £13,097.

The Bear scenario shows what happens if growth slows: roughly £10,920/year in the ISA versus £9,282 in the GIA. The gap between scenarios widens over time because growth compounds — which is why running all three scenarios matters more than fixating on any single projection.

**The wrapper choice alone** accounts for a meaningful difference. In the Base scenario, the ISA portion produces approximately 18% more spendable income than the GIA portion from the same starting capital — entirely because of the dividend tax exemption.

## How to Use the Calculator Alongside Your Existing Dividend Portfolio

The calculator works best as a planning tool, not a one-time check. Here is a practical workflow:

1. **Enter your current portfolio data.** If you track holdings in a spreadsheet or portfolio tracker, use your weighted average yield rather than guessing. Include all wrappers — ISA, SIPP, and GIA — because the tax treatment differs meaningfully.

2. **Run all three scenarios.** Do not skip the Bear scenario. The gap between scenarios grows with time, so a 15-year projection that only shows the Base case gives a false sense of precision.

3. **Compare the post-tax numbers.** The gross projection is less useful than the after-tax figure, especially if you hold a significant portion outside an ISA or SIPP wrapper.

4. **Revisit annually.** Dividend growth rates change, your portfolio composition changes, and tax rules change. The £500 dividend allowance (2026/27) is already lower than previous years, and further changes are possible. An annual check keeps your projections grounded in current rules.
