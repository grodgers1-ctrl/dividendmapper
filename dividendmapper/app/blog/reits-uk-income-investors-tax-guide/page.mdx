Most articles about UK dividend investing focus on equities — dividend-paying stocks listed on the London Stock Exchange. And that makes sense: for most investors, direct equity dividends are the core of an income portfolio.

But there is a second major class of dividend investments that gets far less attention: Real Estate Investment Trusts, or REITs.

REITs work differently from stocks. The tax rules are different. The dividend-sustainability metrics are different. And the wrapper decision — ISA, SIPP, or GIA — matters in a way that catches people out.

This guide covers what UK income investors need to understand about REITs before adding them to a portfolio.

## What a REIT is and why the dividend matters

A REIT is a company that owns and operates income-producing property. UK REITs do not pay corporation tax on their rental income, provided they distribute at least 90% of that tax-exempt profit as dividends to shareholders. The structure was designed to make property investment accessible to retail investors without the capital or legal overhead of owning property directly.

The dividend mechanics flow from that structure. REIT dividends are not optional decisions by a board — the 90% distribution rule means paying out is part of the legal structure. That gives REIT dividends a different profile from equity dividends, which can be cut or suspended more freely when the company needs to conserve cash.

But it also means the sustainability question is different. An equity investor looks at payout ratio and free cash flow. A REIT investor looks at EPRA earnings coverage, loan-to-value ratios, and rent collection rates. More on that below.

## The tax treatment is different from equity dividends

This is the most common point of confusion.

Equity dividends from UK companies come with a £500 dividend allowance (2026/27), then get taxed at 10.75% (basic rate) or 35.75% (higher rate) depending on your tax band. For the full picture on equity dividend tax, see the [UK dividend tax guide](/blog/uk-dividend-tax-guide).

REIT dividends are treated as **property income distributions** (PIDs). They do not qualify for the £500 dividend allowance. Instead, they are taxed as property income at your marginal income tax rate — 20%, 40%, or 45% depending on your total income.

The arithmetic difference matters:

| Scenario | Tax on £2,000 REIT dividends | Tax on £2,000 equity dividends |
|---|---|---|
| Basic-rate taxpayer | £400 (20% of full amount) | £161.25 (10.75% after £500 allowance) |
| Higher-rate taxpayer | £800 (40% of full amount) | £536.25 (35.75% after £500 allowance) |
| Additional-rate taxpayer | £900 (45% of full amount) | £643.75 (35.75% after £500 allowance, no allowance adjustment) |

The gap widens as the amount grows. A higher-rate taxpayer with £10,000 of REIT dividends in a GIA pays £4,000 in tax. The same amount in equity dividends costs £3,397.50 after the £500 allowance.

**What this means for wrapper choice:** REITs in an ISA or SIPP avoid this tax entirely — the wrapper shelters the income. Given that REIT dividends receive less favourable tax treatment than equity dividends in a GIA, the wrapper decision is not optional. You should not hold REITs in a GIA unless you have fully used your ISA and SIPP allowances and have explicitly decided the higher tax cost is worth the diversification.

If you are deciding between wrapper types, the [ISA vs SIPP guide for dividend investors](/blog/isa-vs-sipp-dividend-investors) covers the practical trade-offs.

## Three REIT sectors, three risk profiles

Not all REITs behave the same way. The sector breakdown matters for dividend reliability.

**Residential REITs** own rental housing — build-to-rent blocks, student accommodation, and later-living properties. Demand has been steady through recent cycles because UK housing supply remains constrained and renting is structural rather than discretionary. Yields are typically lower (3-5%) but income is relatively predictable.

**Commercial REITs** own offices, retail, logistics, and industrial property. This is the most cyclical segment. Office REITs faced valuation pressure during the hybrid-work shift. Retail REITs are still adjusting to e-commerce penetration. Logistics and industrial REITs performed strongly through the post-2020 demand surge. Yields are higher (5-7%) but vacancy risk is real.

**Infrastructure REITs** own renewable energy assets, transport infrastructure, data centres, telecom towers, and utilities. These tend to have the longest lease terms (often 15-20 years) and the most inflation-protected income streams because contracts may include CPI or RPI escalators. Yields are in the 4-6% range with generally more predictable income than commercial REITs.

A diversified REIT holding across all three sectors is less risky than a single-sector concentration, but few investors hold enough individual REITs to achieve that diversification themselves. REIT-focused ETFs and investment trusts are the practical route for most portfolios.

## How to evaluate REIT dividend safety

Equity dividend safety checks (payout ratio, free cash flow, debt levels) do not translate directly to REITs. The metrics that matter are different.

If you already use the [dividend safety score framework](/blog/dividend-safety-score-uk-income-investors) for equities, think of this as a separate checklist for a different asset class.

**EPRA earnings coverage** is the closest REIT equivalent to an equity payout ratio. It measures how many times the dividend is covered by underlying earnings, excluding valuation movements. A coverage ratio of 1.2x or higher is generally considered healthy. Below 1.0x means the REIT is paying dividends from capital or reserves, which is not sustainable.

**Loan-to-value ratio** measures how much the REIT borrows relative to its property assets. Most UK REITs target 30-45% LTV. Above 50% starts to look stretched, especially in a rising interest rate environment where refinancing costs eat into distributable income.

**Interest cover ratio** tells you whether the REIT's operating income comfortably covers its borrowing costs. Below 2.0x is a warning sign — less than £2 of operating profit for every £1 of interest. Rising rates compress interest cover across the sector.

**Rent collection rates** are a simple but powerful indicator. A REIT collecting 98%+ of rents due is in good shape. A REIT reporting 90% or lower has structural occupancy or tenant-quality problems.

**Weighted average lease term (WALE)** tells you how long the current rental income is contracted for. A WALE of 8+ years gives income visibility. A WALE of 3 years or less means a lot of lease expiry risk in the near term.

None of these metrics alone tells you enough. A REIT with strong coverage but very short WALE could look healthy today and face a cliff-edge next year.

## The yield trap is alive in REITs

The standard advice not to chase yield applies to REITs even more strongly than to equities. A REIT yielding 7%+ while the sector median is 5% is not automatically a bargain. It may be pricing in known asset stress, a pending dividend cut, or structural sector weakness.

The same principle from [why headline yield can be misleading](/blog/why-headline-yield-can-be-misleading) applies: check the coverage, check the LTV, check the WALE. A high yield that is not covered by EPRA earnings is a dividend that will be cut.

## What this means for your portfolio

REITs are a legitimate diversifier for a UK income portfolio. They offer exposure to a different income stream than equities, with different tax mechanics and a different risk profile. In an ISA or SIPP, the tax disadvantage of PIDs disappears entirely, making REITs a straightforward income addition.

But they are not a set-and-forget asset class. The sector mix matters. The individual REIT's coverage and leverage metrics matter. And the wrapper decision matters more than it does for equities because the tax gap between GIA-held REITs and wrapper-held REITs is wide.

If you are building a dividend portfolio from scratch, the natural starting point is equity dividends in a wrapper-efficient mix of ISA and SIPP. As the portfolio grows, REITs are worth considering as a secondary income layer — but only after the wrapper question is answered.
